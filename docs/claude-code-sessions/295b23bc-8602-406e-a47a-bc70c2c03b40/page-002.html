Question 3: "Last 36 months" — how do you actually get coverage across the window, not just a filter on it?

The spec says: ~6–8 queries "with date filters for 36 months." Two distinct problems hide in there:

  1. Filtering (keeping the promise): Tavily supports start_date/end_date params and returns published_date reliably only on topic: "news" queries — general-topic results often have no date at all. You've handled the output side ("date unknown" flag). Fine. (I'd verify the exact param behaviour during slice 2 — it's an API-docs fact, not a design choice.)

  2. Coverage (the actual brief: "as much content as possible from the last 36 months"): search APIs are heavily recency-and-popularity biased. One "Acme news" query returns its top ~20 hits, which will skew to the last few months. Nothing in the current design pulls 2023–2024 content toward you. A reviewer who scans the results and sees nothing older than 6 months may judge the 36-month requirement unmet — even though every result technically passes the filter.

Options:

a) Accept the bias, name it in the write-up. 8 queries, first page each, whatever dates come back. b) Time-slice the highest-yield query type only. The news query becomes 3 queries (one per 12-month window via start_date/end_date). Adds ~2 credits/job, materially spreads coverage where dates are reliable. c) Time-slice everything. 8 queries × 3 windows = 24 queries. Blows the budget cap.

My recommendation: (b). It's the cost-aware version of taking the requirement literally: ~2 extra credits buys visible 36-month spread in the results list, which is exactly what the evaluator will scan for. (a) is defensible but you'd be writing "we didn't really try" in the write-up's weakest section; (c) violates your own budget reasoning. And (b) gives the write-up a nice line: "time-slicing generalises to all query types as recall demands grow."

One dependent sub-decision if you take (b): the per-12-month news queries should also dedup against each other — already handled by your per-job URL unique constraint, so no extra work. Agree with (b)?

Question 4: What happens to a job that's mid-flight when the process dies?

Your spec creates a tension: jobs are durable (SQLite rows) but the queue is not (in-process, and "queue durability across restart" is explicitly out of scope). Concrete scenario: job is in searching, you ctrl-C the dev server, restart it. The job row still says searching. The UI dutifully polls it every 2 seconds. Forever. The evaluator who restarts the app — and they will, it's a clone-and-run exercise — sees a zombie job spinning eternally. That reads as a bug, not a scoping decision.

Options:

a) Nothing — accept the zombie, it's documented as out of scope. b) Boot-time sweep — on startup, mark every job in a non-terminal state as failed with message "interrupted by restart". ~3 lines of SQL. c) Boot-time resume — re-enqueue interrupted jobs from their last completed stage. Real durability, real work: stage outputs must be persisted and idempotent.

My recommendation: (b). "Out of scope" should mean we don't resume jobs, not the UI hangs forever. The sweep is three lines, turns the failure mode into an honest, visible state, and the failure message itself demonstrates that you thought about process lifecycle — cheap signal on exactly the criterion being graded. (c) is the genuinely correct production answer and belongs in the write-up as the next step ("the state machine already gives us resume points; persistence of intermediate stage output is the missing piece"), but it's an hour you don't have.

Also note (b) interacts nicely with your earlier answer: the future cancel button (Q1's option c) and the boot sweep both need the same thing — a way to transition a job to a terminal state from outside the pipeline. Worth shaping the state machine API for that now: a single transition(jobId, toState, message) rather than stage-internal-only updates.

Agree with (b)?

Question 5: Two jobs submitted — serial or concurrent?

The "tiny async queue" needs a concurrency policy. Scenario: the evaluator starts a job for "Stripe", immediately gets curious and starts one for "Notion". Now: do both pipelines run interleaved, or does Notion wait in pending until Stripe finishes?

Options:

a) Concurrency 1 — strict FIFO. Second job sits visibly in pending. Simplest possible queue; one job's API spend at a time. b) Unbounded — every job runs the moment it's created. The "queue" is just Promise bookkeeping. c) Small cap (2–3) — real queue semantics with a worker-pool flavour.

My recommendation: (a). Three reasons: (1) a visible pending → resolving transition demonstrates the queue exists — with (b) nobody can tell your queue from a bare async call, and background-job design is being graded; (2) it serialises API spend, which is your cost story made concrete; (3) it's the honest miniature of the BullMQ upgrade path you're already claiming in the trade-offs table — BullMQ workers have a concurrency setting, your queue has 1 hardcoded. (b) is less code but undermines the narrative; (c) is realism you can't demo with one user.

Only caveat on (a): with jobs taking ~30–60s, a queued second job waits a noticeable while — fine for the demo, and the status page makes the wait legible.
