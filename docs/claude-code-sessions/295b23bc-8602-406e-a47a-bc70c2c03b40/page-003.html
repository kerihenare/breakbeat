Question 7: Are the brief's seven content types your taxonomy verbatim, or do you adapt them?

The brief lists: news articles, trade publications, blog posts, press releases, major social posts, newsletters, podcasts. Tempting to treat that as the enum. But stress-test it on title+snippet+URL — which is all Haiku gets:

  • News vs trade publication: "Acme raises $50M" on TechCrunch vs on Supply Chain Dive. The distinction is a property of the outlet, not the content — and Haiku-from-snippet will guess inconsistently.
  • Newsletter: a Substack post about Acme is... a newsletter? A blog post? Both are defensible; the evaluator's guess is as good as Haiku's.
  • Things that fit nothing: a conference talk recording, a YouTube review-of-the-industry mentioning Acme, a podcast transcript page.

Options:

a) The brief's 7, verbatim, + other. Maximum legibility against the brief; accept fuzzy boundaries and let the confidence flag absorb the ambiguity. b) Merge the fuzzy pairs — e.g. news/trade, blog/newsletter, press release, social, podcast, other. More honest about what's distinguishable from a snippet. c) The 7 + other, but boundary rules in the prompt — verbatim taxonomy, with explicit tie-breakers ("trade publication = industry-niche outlet; if unsure between news and trade, choose news; newsletter = email-first publication, Substack counts").

My recommendation: (c). The evaluator will map your groupings straight back to their bullet list — (b) makes them work to check compliance, which is the wrong place to spend their goodwill. But (a) without boundary rules produces visibly inconsistent grouping in the demo (same outlet appearing under two types), which undermines "result quality." The tie-breaker rules cost five lines of prompt and double as evidence you saw the ambiguity — there's a "handling ambiguity" criterion with your name on it. Keep other as the explicit escape hatch so Haiku never force-fits; other + low-confidence is a reviewable answer, which is what the list is for.

Question 8: When two results collapse on normalized title, which one wins — and what stops false collapses?

"Near-dup collapse on normalized title" leaves two decisions unstated:

Winner rule. Scenario: "Acme Raises $50M Series B" appears on TechCrunch (Mar 12), Yahoo Finance (Mar 12, syndicated), and MSN (Mar 13, syndicated). All three survive heuristics. The list should show one. Which?

  • a) First-seen — whatever insertion order produced. Arbitrary but free.
  • b) Earliest published date — proxy for the original source; syndication copies follow the original. Falls back to first-seen when dates are missing.
  • c) Domain authority ranking — a curated "prefer these outlets" list. Real products do this; you don't have time to curate it.

False-collapse guard. Normalized titles can collide without being duplicates: "Acme" (two different one-word titles), "Acme — Wikipedia"-style truncations, or two genuinely different articles titled "Acme launches new product" eighteen months apart.

My recommendation: (b) for the winner, recorded as excluded, reason: "duplicate of #N" on the losers — which your soft-exclusion decision makes free and visible (the evaluator literally sees dedup working in the excluded section — that's the "deduplication" criterion demoed, not described). For the guard, two cheap rules: only collapse titles ≥ ~25 chars after normalization, and only when published dates are within ~14 days of each other (or either date is unknown). Syndication happens within days; same-title-18-months-apart is two stories.

The 14-day window also resolves a subtle interaction with your time-sliced queries: the same story appearing in two adjacent 12-month windows (boundary straddle) still collapses, because the dates are close even if the windows differ.

Agree — (b) + the two guards?

# Breakbeat

Finds third-party content about a company from the last 36 months via a background pipeline, and presents it as a reviewable list. Single context.

## Language

**Job**:
One run of the pipeline for one company, moving through a state machine from `pending` to a terminal state.
_Avoid_: task, search (a Job *contains* a Search stage)

**Resolved Identity**:
The company name, own domains, and scraped social handles established by the Resolve stage; the anchor every later stage filters against.
_Avoid_: company profile, match

**Own Channel**:
Content published *by* the company — its domains, or its accounts on third-party platforms. Always excluded. Content *about* the company on those same platforms is in scope.
_Avoid_: own site (too narrow — misses social accounts)

**Result**:
One fetched search hit, stored permanently with a `status` of `included` or `excluded`.
_Avoid_: hit, item, link

**Exclusion**:
Marking a Result `excluded` with a human-readable reason. Soft — never a delete; nothing is dropped except by never being fetched.
_Avoid_: drop, delete, filter out (as a verb for the action)

**Collapse**:
Deduplicating near-identical Results on normalized title; the winner is the earliest-published copy, losers are Excluded as duplicates.
_Avoid_: merge, dedupe (reserve "dedup" for the stage name)

**Content Type**:
The brief's seven categories verbatim (news article, trade publication, blog post, press release, major social post, newsletter, podcast) plus `other` as the explicit escape hatch.

**Angle Query**:
A search query phrased around an event type ("X funding", "X acquisition") rather than a content type — recall comes from more angles, not more slices.

**Time Slice**:
One 12-month `start_date`/`end_date` window of a query; applied only to news and press releases, where dates are reliable.

## Relationships

- A **Job** belongs to one company and produces many **Results**
- A **Job**'s Resolve stage produces one **Resolved Identity**; Search, Filter, and Classify all consume it
- A **Collapse** Excludes all but one of a set of near-duplicate **Results**
- Every **Result** is assigned one **Content Type** (by heuristics or by the Classify stage)

## Example dialogue

> **Dev:** "The heuristic pass dropped a bunch of Reddit links."
> **Domain expert:** "Nothing is *dropped* — they're **Excluded** as aggregators, with the reason on the row. They'll show in the collapsed excluded section."
> **Dev:** "And the company's LinkedIn posts?"
> **Domain expert:** "**Own Channel**, Excluded. But a journalist's LinkedIn post *about* the company is in scope — Own Channel is about the author, not the platform."

## Flagged ambiguities

- "drop" was used for both *never fetched* and *filtered out* — resolved: filtered-out Results are **Excluded** (soft, with reason); only unfetched content is absent.
- "own site" vs **Own Channel** — resolved: exclusion covers the company's accounts on third-party platforms, not just its domains, and never third-party content on those platforms.
